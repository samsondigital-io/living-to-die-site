## When Women's Voices Go Unheard

Three words changed everything: "It looks clear."

The radiologist's confident declaration about Brenda Sawicki's mammogram in August 2000 would prove to be devastatingly wrong. But the tragedy wasn't just in the missed diagnosis—it was in the system that made questioning that diagnosis so difficult, so fraught, so easily dismissed.

As I wrote *Living to Die*, Brenda's story forced me to confront an uncomfortable truth: the medical establishment still struggles to hear women's voices, particularly when those voices challenge professional judgment.

### The Pattern of Dismissal

Brenda's experience wasn't unique. Research consistently shows that women's pain is more likely to be dismissed as emotional or psychological. We're told we're being "dramatic" or "anxious" when we insist something is wrong. Our symptoms are often attributed to stress, hormones, or "all in our heads."

When Brenda felt the lump that the mammogram supposedly didn't show, she faced a choice that too many women know: trust the technology and the experts, or trust her own body. She chose to keep fighting, keep questioning, keep demanding answers.

### The Cost of Being Right

But being right came at a devastating cost. By the time her cancer was properly diagnosed, it had progressed far beyond what it would have been in August 2000. The legal battle that followed—eight years of depositions, expert witnesses, and courtroom drama—was never just about money. It was about accountability.

"I want to make sure this doesn't happen to other women," Brenda told me during one of our interviews. "If my story can save one person from going through this, then all of this will have been worth it."

### What Needs to Change

Writing Brenda's story taught me that meaningful change requires multiple shifts:

**In Medical Training**: Healthcare providers need explicit training in unconscious bias and patient advocacy. The patient's voice should be treated as crucial data, not background noise.

**In Documentation**: When patients express concerns that contradict test results, those concerns should be thoroughly documented and investigated, not dismissed.

**In Legal Standards**: The medical malpractice system needs to better account for the long-term consequences of missed diagnoses, particularly when patients have repeatedly expressed concerns.

### A Call to Action

Every woman reading this likely has a story of not being heard in a healthcare setting. Maybe it was minor—a concern brushed off that turned out to be nothing. Maybe it was major—a serious condition that went undiagnosed because your symptoms didn't fit the textbook.

Brenda's legacy isn't just in the legal precedent her case helped establish. It's in the reminder that we must be our own advocates, even when—especially when—it's uncomfortable to question authority.

The next time you're in a medical setting and something doesn't feel right, remember Brenda. Ask questions. Demand explanations. Get second opinions. Trust your body.

And if you're a healthcare provider reading this, remember that the voice of your patient—especially when it contradicts your initial assessment—may be the most important diagnostic tool you have.

*Diane Melton is the author of "Living to Die: A True Story of Medical Negligence and One Family's Fight for Justice," forthcoming in 2024.*
